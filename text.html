INFSTD 270 Dossier: Digital Asset Management

Emily Popejoy, Isabella New-Walker, Lola Wegman, Matthew Dessem

Overview

Our group looked into the current landscape for digital asset management and preservation jobs in digital archiving. To learn more about this aspect of the LIS field, we interviewed Linda Tadic, the CEO and founder of Digital Bedrock, a firm that offers digital preservation services to clients including film studios, artists and filmmakers, GLAM institutions, government agencies, and personal archives.

Tadic’s career path followed the growth of the field: from an analog start in the mid 80s, through the rapid transition to the digital age in the early 2000s, to CEO of a company that works exclusively with digital files. Her trajectory was far from linear but reflects the strengths a diverse set of experiences can provide. Tadic was first exposed to cataloging while studying experimental film at CalArts where she worked at the library during its transition from analog to digital cataloging and learned MARC encoding, her first “saleable” skill, as she describes it. Her combination of technical cataloging skills and film background provided a unique advantage in the field, ultimately leading to her positions at institutions such as the Pacific Film Archive, the UCLA Film & Television Archive, the Peabody Awards Archives, HBO, the Getty, and ARTstor, before founding Digital Bedrock to offer professional digital asset management.

Though digital asset management is playing an increasingly important role in the LIS fields, an MLIS degree is not generally required. Instead, according to Tadic, it is more important to have a solid conceptual understanding of the technology used in the field—scripting, storage media, LINUX/UNIX—than any particular degree, and this is reflected in her hiring practices. The most important thing for MLIS students to understand about the field is that employers are looking for practical skills and the ability to learn, not credentials. An aptitude in critical thinking is far more valued than experience with AI tools which can introduce significant and unnecessary security risks while handling digital files, particularly for a company such as Digital Bedrock which is built around ensuring the protection of client data.

Typical Tasks

Though the general responsibilities of a DAM professional vary by position and seniority, the most common tasks include:

  1. Designing and managing workflow for digital asset lifecycle
  2. Metadata creation, schema, and tagging
  3. Asset ingestion, maintenance, and quality control
  4. Governing access and control (who can access what/when/how, and what changes are made)
  5. Training DAM users, onboarding clients and employees, and troubleshooting support
  6. System administration, maintenance and audits

Here are examples of digital asset management job listings at three levels of seniority, demonstrating the wide variety of tasks and responsibilities found in the field.

Professional Context

The field of digital asset management is rapidly growing and changing, but a variety of journals and professional organizations help practitioners stay up to date. Two of the largest news sources for DAM coverage are DAMNews, (dedicated to digital asset management specifically) and CMSWire (a publication with a broader focus that includes a digital asset management channel). Although prominent DAM-specific journals like DAM Guru and Journal of Digital Asset Management are no longer publishing, related articles can be found in the Journal of Digital Media Management or in journals of computer science, media and other adjacent fields.

Professional associations are hugely important in a field that values experience more than education. Linda Tadic repeatedly emphasised her long involvement with the Association of Moving Image Archivists (AMIA), a critical part of the landscape. Other relevant professional associations include:

Job Market Outlook

Though the reach of digital asset management is already quite expansive, it is only continuing to grow, spurred by factors including continued content production, analog format obsolescence, and shifts in DAM technology and standards. As digital asset managers are also preservationists, they play a crucial and ongoing role in adjusting to technological change and all that comes with it: security improvements, data asset migration, upgrading databases and user platforms, etc.

The field is currently in flux as GLAM spaces adjust to the collapse of federal funding, but a DAM News report from last March, “Results of the Fourth Digital Asset Management Salary Survey,” gives an overview of labor trends and changes over the past decade. The survey reported that wages have risen overall while the pay gap that previously existed between men and women has decreased significantly. In fact, the field currently comprises more women than men. Though there has been a 10% increase in people of color over the last decade, the field remains predominantly white, specifically dominated by white women. While there is limited discourse surrounding this fact, DAM committees and associations have been proactive in considering Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) efforts in their work, as we can see in the Collaborative DEI Controlled Vocabulary Resource List (AMIA) and The DEI Metadata Handbook: A Guide to Diverse, Equitable, and Inclusive Description.

Technical Skill Snapshot

Across most listings for entry-level jobs a “working knowledge” of the field is encouraged (a.k.a. “willingness to learn”) and skill requirements are limited and rarely specified by name in job listings. Ms. Tadic and other professionals in the field, mentioned the following skills as particularly desirable:

  • Basic familiarity with digital file formats, digital preservation practices, digital workflows and any and all varieties of data storage.
  • An understanding of taxonomies and metadata standards such as popular schemas, controlled vocabularies, and workflows for categorization and organization.
  • Basic scripting. These skills can be expanded with on-the-job training, but most applicants know one or more of the following:
    • Data manipulation in Excel or other spreadsheet programs.
    • Python, the most commonly used language for digital archiving/preservation
    • Parsing data with automated commands
    • More advanced technical skills, such as proficiency in SQL
  • People Skills:
    • Strong client communication, even with remote positions
    • Collaborating with team members and across departments and training new hires
    • Managing user requests and client-facing troubleshooting
  • Project management and organizational skills

Tadic noted that proficiency in specific programs and standards were not necessarily as important as general knowledge, as institutions often use different, or even proprietary, systems. Instead, she emphasized competency in basic scripting, particularly Python, which can be transferred to any system. These skills can be self-taught, or learned in an educational setting like UCLA’s Office of Advanced Research Computing workshops, which offer live and recorded training courses. Foundational programming or technical knowledge helps show employers that an applicant has a baseline understanding of these skills that can be built upon to suit institution-specific needs.

It is important to note that acquiring an MLIS or LIS certification is not necessarily a requirement for jobs in this field. Although an MLIS is the most common graduate degree in the field, the Digital Asset Management Salary Survey found that there is no correlation between pay and level of education. Some higher-level positions, such as the Senior Manager, Digital Assets and Licensing position at the Guggenheim, only require a high school diploma. As an employer, Ms. Tadic explained that she “doesn’t ask for a degree because she wants to be open to candidates with multiple types of backgrounds and experiences.” A willingness to learn and aptitude for critical and creative thinking is more important to employers in this field than concrete, on-paper skills.

AI in DAM

As AI technology advances and the DAM field evolves their increasing interaction is inevitable, though the benefits of this interaction must be considered alongside its potential dangers. For Tadic, these dangers far outweigh any possible advantages, and so she prohibits the use of AI at Digital Bedrock, believing that “smart coding” can complete a task as efficiently as an AI tool. It is important to note that Digital Bedrock is not indicative of the DAM field as a whole as it revolves around automated processes for secure storage of client files instead of content analysis, though it is indicative of both institutional and client attitudes towards AI in DAM. Tadic emphasized that her clients almost unanimously opposed AI interaction with their files, recognizing the dramatic increase in risk of security breaches it could introduce.

When asked about general AI use in the DAM field, Tadic’s response was clear: “Oh, everybody’s using AI.” She noted that at a tech retreat she attended through the Hollywood Professional Association, AI dominated the conversation, with topics spanning from generative content creation to file analysis. Though AI will undoubtedly remain relevant in the field, its use may be overstated as a trending topic. Tadic provides an important reminder that much of what we are turning to AI to complete can easily, or already has been, solved. As she puts it: “use [your] brain and do it.” With most DAM job listings across institutions emphasizing the importance of problem solving, collaborative skills, and creative thinking, Tadic is clearly not alone in this mindset.

Training Agenda

To demonstrate these foundational skills and show employers your willingness to learn, we recommend taking free, beginner level courses to learn scripting languages such as Python or SQL. Many live workshops, recorded courses, and guided learning programs can be found online; a few suggested resources include Google Python, Coursera’s Python for Everybody Specialization, IBM’s Data Science Professional Certificate, and LearnPython.org. It is also strongly recommended to apply to DAM internships for hands-on experience and stay informed via the professional organizations and resources mentioned in the Professional Context section.

Labor Trends

Digital asset management professionals are in high demand and so current jobs often offer a great deal of flexibility and high professional mobility, though pay often depends on experience, skill and position level. ZipRecruiter reports the average pay for a digital asset manager to be around $95,700 a year. Entry level salaries range from $55,000 to $70,000, with contract positions paying around $55 to $60 per hour. As the average salary is almost double the baseline entry level pay, employment positions hold the potential for significant growth.

Regarding work style, the authors of the DAM salary survey noted that “one of the largest changes… was the decline in ‘Lone Arrangers’,” a “big shift in DAMs operated by teams rather than individuals [which] likely points to a shift towards more collaborative work in this professional space.” Though collaborative work is clearly a focus in the field, many job opportunities are remote-work friendly—in fact, remote-work is often encouraged, with 46% of managers reporting that they were remote-work only.

Since labor trends in the field of digital access management are generally trending in the direction of higher pay and more flexibility, there is little organized resistance of these trends. However, as with every other field in the LIS space, the situation is in flux.

Although this directory is not incredibly up to date, it is still an incredibly useful tool with relevant information and resources divided up into 10 category-specific guides. Due to its selective content, simplicity, and interactive and visually-appealing format, this website is a good jumping off point for those who don’t want to be inundated with tons of information at once.

This article is a brief, but useful overview of the digital asset manager role. Whereas most articles about DAM specifically detail digital asset management systems rather than managers, this article stands out by providing an in-depth description of the role itself and why managers are important for companies to successfully maintain their DAM systems and workflows. It also provides ample advice on how to best navigate and prepare for the DAM job market.
